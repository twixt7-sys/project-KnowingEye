advancement of digital technologies has significantly transformed how institutions conduct assessments, certifications, and competency evaluations. Online and computer-based examination systems have become essential tools for educational institutions, government agencies, and training organizations seeking efficient, scalable, and accessible methods of evaluating examinees. As assessment environments increasingly shift toward digital platforms, ensuring examination integrity, fairness, and reliability has emerged as a critical concern. While online examinations provide convenience and automation, they also introduce challenges in monitoring examinee behavior and preventing dishonest practices that were traditionally controlled through physical supervision.Globally, the adoption of web-based examination platforms has accelerated alongside digital transformation initiatives in education and professional certification sectors. Modern examination systems enable automated test administration, centralized record management, and remote accessibility, allowing institutions to conduct assessments across geographically distributed participants. However, studies emphasize that maintaining academic integrity remains one of the primary limitations of remote assessments, as human invigilation alone cannot effectively supervise large numbers of examinees simultaneously in virtual environments. Consequently, researchers have explored artificial intelligence and computer vision technologies as mechanisms for enhancing examination monitoring through automated behavioral analysis and identity verification.Recent developments in artificial intelligence-based proctoring systems demonstrate the growing role of computer vision and deep learning in examination monitoring. The iExam system implemented face detection and recognition to monitor students during online examinations, achieving 90.4% face detection accuracy and 98.4% face recognition accuracy while identifying abnormal behaviors such as face disappearance and identity changes [1]. Similarly, the AutoOEP multi-modal online examination proctoring framework integrated gaze tracking, head pose estimation, and object detection to compute cheating probability in real time using machine learning models [2]. Together, these studies indicate a transition from simple identity verification toward continuous behavioral monitoring, where multiple visual indicators are analyzed simultaneously to improve examination supervision accuracy. These approaches highlight how automated monitoring can supplement human supervision by continuously analyzing behavioral indicators during examinations.Other research further supports the effectiveness of AI-assisted proctoring approaches. The Talent-Interview system introduced client-side cheating detection operating directly within a web browser, combining face recognition, object detection, and voice monitoring to identify suspicious activities locally on the examinee’s device [3]. Meanwhile, artificial intelligence-based online examination monitoring systems have demonstrated the capability to automatically flag suspicious behaviors and record supporting evidence for later review by human proctors, improving monitoring efficiency while reducing manual workload [4]. Additional studies utilizing computer vision techniques reported facial verification accuracy of 94.2% and head movement detection accuracy of 89.7%, reinforcing the reliability of behavioral analysis technologies in assessment environments [5]. Collectively, these findings suggest that modern proctoring systems are evolving toward automated assistance models in which artificial intelligence augments, rather than replaces, human judgment during examinations.Advancements in object detection models such as YOLO have further enhanced real-time monitoring capabilities by enabling detection of facial orientation, mouth movement, and prohibited objects such as mobile phones during examinations [6]. Multi-perspective monitoring approaches using gaze recognition and multiple camera inputs have also been explored to strengthen cheating detection accuracy and behavioral analysis reliability [7]. These developments demonstrate an emerging research trend emphasizing contextual behavioral understanding, where examination monitoring expands beyond presence detection toward interpreting examinee actions and interaction patterns. Collectively, prior studies confirm that integrating computer vision and deep learning techniques into examination systems can significantly improve monitoring accuracy and support fair evaluation processes.Despite these technological advancements, many institutions continue to rely on partially integrated solutions that separate examination delivery from monitoring mechanisms. Environmental scanning conducted for this study revealed that the Land Transportation Office (LTO) already utilizes a computer-based examination system supported by face tracking and identity verification features. However, suspicious behavior detection still largely depends on closed-circuit television (CCTV) monitoring or assigned personnel observing examinees manually. During large-scale examinations, human proctors experience cognitive limitations that make continuous monitoring of all examinees difficult, increasing the likelihood of missed suspicious behaviors, inconsistent supervision, and delayed intervention.Furthermore, several existing examination platforms primarily focus on assessment administration functions such as question management and automated scoring while providing only passive monitoring approaches, including webcam recording or screen locking. These systems do not actively analyze behavioral patterns in real time, limiting their ability to detect abnormal actions related to gaze direction, posture changes, or facial movement. The absence of integrated behavioral analytics increases reliance on manual review processes and reduces monitoring efficiency.Given these conditions, a significant gap exists in the development of an integrated examination platform that combines examination management and intelligent behavioral monitoring within a unified system. Current solutions often emphasize either examination delivery or monitoring independently, resulting in fragmented workflows and limited analytical support for administrators. There is therefore a need for a centralized platform capable of conducting examinations while simultaneously analyzing examinee behavior through automated computer vision techniques.To address these challenges, this study proposes Knowing Eye: A Full-Stack Session-Guided Web-Based Examination Platform Application with Integrated Behavior Monitoring System Using Facial and Postural Analysis via Computer Vision. The proposed system integrates examination creation, administration, and monitoring within a single web-based environment. Utilizing computer vision and deep learning algorithms such as YOLO for detection, convolutional neural networks (CNN) for feature extraction, and facial embedding models including FaceNet or ArcFace for facial consistency verification, the system analyzes head movement, eye gaze, and posture in real time to identify behavioral anomalies during examination sessions. A behavior scoring engine generates analytical reports that assist administrators in reviewing examination integrity efficiently and objectively.The study aligns with the Commission on Higher Education (CHED) ACHIEVE Framework by promoting innovation, digital transformation, and the application of emerging technologies to real-world institutional challenges. By integrating artificial intelligence, computer vision, and full-stack web development into assessment systems, the research demonstrates how academic innovation can enhance transparency, efficiency, and credibility in modern examination environments used by government agencies, training institutions, and higher education organizations.For clarity, several key terms are defined within the context of this study. Online examination platforms refer to web-based systems used to create, administer, and evaluate assessments digitally. Computer vision denotes artificial intelligence techniques that enable computers to interpret visual information from images or video streams. Behavioral monitoring refers to the automated analysis of examinee actions such as facial movement, gaze direction, and posture to identify potential irregularities during examinations. Deep learning describes neural network-based algorithms capable of extracting complex patterns from visual data for recognition and classification tasks.Ultimately, this study responds to the growing demand for secure, reliable, and intelligent online assessment systems. By integrating behavioral monitoring with examination management functionalities, the proposed platform aims to reduce reliance on manual supervision, enhance examination integrity, and support fair and transparent evaluation processes in contemporary digital learning and certification environments.REFERENCES:[1] iExam: Online Exam Monitoring Using Face Detection. The proposed system aims to provide a centralized platform that enables examination creation, administration, and automated behavioral monitoring to support secure and credible online assessments.The study is intended for institutions that conduct computer-based examinations, including government agencies, training institutions, and higher education organizations such as the Land Transportation Office (LTO), Technical Education and Skills Development Authority (TESDA), and selected higher educational institutions. Environmental scanning data are used to understand existing examination workflows and monitoring practices, particularly the reliance on manual supervision despite the presence of computer-based testing systems.The locale of the study is limited to institutional examination environments where online or computer-based assessments are conducted. The system is evaluated within an academic development setting using simulated examination sessions and controlled testing conditions rather than large-scale institutional deployment.Data utilized in the study consist of video streams captured through examinees’ web cameras during examination sessions, system-generated behavioral logs, and examination interaction records. Publicly available datasets are used to support facial and posture detection model training, while additional controlled datasets are collected through recorded mock examination sessions with participant consent. These datasets are used solely for model training, testing, and performance evaluation purposes.The population covered by the study includes two primary user groups:• Exam takers, who participate in online examinations monitored through webcam-based behavioral analysis.• Exam administrators, who create examinations, monitor sessions, and review behavioral reports generated by the system.The development process covers the phases of system analysis, design, implementation, testing, and evaluation within the academic timeline of the capstone project.The proposed system includes the following functional coverage:• Web-based examination creation and management• Session-guided online examination delivery• Real-time webcam capture during examination sessions• Facial detection and posture analysis using computer vision techniques• Behavioral monitoring and anomaly flagging through a scoring engine• Administrative dashboard for reviewing examination sessions and reports• Storage of examination logs and monitoring recordsThe study is limited to a web-based implementation using desktop or laptop environments equipped with webcams. Mobile application deployment, integration with external institutional databases, biometric hardware devices, and nationwide operational deployment are beyond the scope of this research. Additionally, the system provides behavioral indicators rather than definitive accusations of cheating, with final judgment remaining under administrator discretion.---Objectives of the StudyGeneral ObjectiveThis study aims to design and develop Knowing Eye, a full-stack web-based examination platform integrated with computer vision-based behavioral monitoring using facial and postural analysis to enhance examination integrity, monitoring efficiency, and assessment reliability.Specifically, this study seeks to:1.

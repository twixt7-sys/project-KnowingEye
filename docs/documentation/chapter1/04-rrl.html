Chapter I

Review of Related Literature and Studies

Aligned with Draft Outline Paper 2 — RRL section (pp. 8–14).

This section presents the review of related literature and related studies relevant to the Web-Based Examination Platform with Behavior Monitoring Using Facial and Postural Analysis.

Related Literature

Artificial intelligence (AI) has become an important technology for improving the security and credibility of online examinations. According to Nigam et al. [8], AI-based proctoring systems employ computer vision and machine learning to automate identity verification and monitor examinee behavior throughout an assessment. Their review emphasized that facial recognition, gaze estimation, and head pose analysis are among the most widely adopted techniques because they enable continuous monitoring while reducing the workload of human invigilators.

Building on these developments, Nafrees et al. [9] explained that deep learning models, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), have significantly improved the accuracy of facial recognition compared with traditional image-processing methods. Their review found that CNN-based approaches provide reliable face detection even under varying lighting conditions and facial orientations, making them suitable for real-time online examination systems.

As online assessments become more common, researchers have also highlighted the importance of balancing examination security with ethical considerations. Han et al. [10] reported that digital proctoring technologies have strengthened academic integrity in higher education through automated identity verification and behavioral monitoring. However, the authors stressed that developers should ensure transparency, data privacy, fairness, and accessibility when implementing AI-powered examination systems.

Beyond identity verification, intelligent proctoring systems increasingly rely on behavioral analysis to identify suspicious activities during examinations. Kaddoura and Gumaei [11] proposed that integrating facial recognition with head pose estimation and eye gaze tracking improves cheating detection because multiple behavioral indicators provide more reliable evidence than a single monitoring technique. Their work demonstrated that multimodal computer vision significantly enhances the performance of automated examination monitoring.

Student posture has also emerged as an important behavioral indicator in online assessments. Shrivas and Sonawane [12] demonstrated that combining body posture analysis with facial monitoring allows intelligent systems to recognize abnormal movements that may indicate suspicious behavior. Their findings showed that multimodal behavioral analysis produces more accurate monitoring results than facial recognition alone.

Similarly, Jadhav and Mehta [13] emphasized the growing importance of eye gaze estimation in AI-powered online examination platforms. Their review showed that deep learning-based gaze tracking enables systems to determine whether students maintain visual attention on the examination screen, making it an effective indicator for detecting potential cheating behavior while maintaining real-time performance.

While technical improvements continue to enhance monitoring accuracy, ethical concerns remain an important consideration. Miranda et al. [14] argued that AI-based educational surveillance systems should incorporate privacy-preserving mechanisms, explainable artificial intelligence, and responsible data management to ensure that automated proctoring remains fair, transparent, and acceptable to students and educational institutions.

Finally, Holden et al. [15] concluded that technology alone cannot completely eliminate academic dishonesty but can substantially strengthen examination integrity when integrated with institutional policies and appropriate assessment practices. Their review suggested that combining intelligent monitoring technologies with sound academic regulations provides the most effective approach to maintaining fairness in online examinations.

Related Studies

Recent studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of artificial intelligence in improving the security of online examination systems through automated behavior monitoring. The iExam system [1] utilizes face detection and recognition to monitor students, achieving 90.4% face detection accuracy and 98.4% face recognition accuracy while detecting abnormal behaviors such as face disappearance and identity changes. Similarly, the AutoOEP framework [2] combines gaze tracking, head pose estimation, and object detection to compute cheating probability in real time, demonstrating a shift from simple identity verification to continuous behavioral monitoring using multiple visual indicators.

The Talent-Interview system [3] performs cheating detection directly on the client side using face recognition, object detection, and voice monitoring. AI-based proctoring research [4] shows that systems can automatically flag suspicious behavior and record evidence for review by human proctors. Another study reports 94.2% facial verification accuracy and 89.7% head movement detection accuracy using computer vision techniques [5]. YOLO-based systems [6] enable object detection models to identify facial orientation, mouth movement, and prohibited objects such as mobile phones during exams. Furthermore, multi-perspective monitoring approaches [7] show that using multiple cameras and gaze recognition improves detection accuracy and provides better analysis of examinee behavior.

Tiong and Lee [16] developed an intelligent online examination proctoring system that employed deep learning models to detect suspicious behaviors during remote examinations. Their evaluation showed that the DenseLSTM model achieved approximately 95% detection accuracy, indicating that deep learning can effectively reduce academic dishonesty while minimizing the need for continuous human supervision.

To improve the efficiency of reviewing examination recordings, Li et al. [17] introduced a visual analytics framework that automatically analyzes webcam videos and user interactions during online assessments. Instead of manually reviewing lengthy recordings, instructors were provided with summarized behavioral events and highlighted suspicious activities. The study demonstrated that visual analytics can significantly reduce review time while improving the identification of possible examination violations.

Recognizing the limitations of single-feature monitoring, Noorbehbahani et al. [18] investigated various cheating detection approaches used in online examinations. Their study concluded that combining facial recognition, eye gaze tracking, browser activity monitoring, and identity verification provides higher reliability than relying on a single behavioral indicator. The researchers recommended adopting multimodal monitoring frameworks to strengthen examination integrity.

Similarly, Singh and Das [19] developed a computer vision-based cheating detection system that combined eye gaze estimation with head pose analysis. Experimental results showed that integrating these two behavioral features improved the detection of suspicious activities, such as repeatedly looking away from the examination screen and unusual head movements. The study demonstrated that multiple behavioral indicators provide more accurate monitoring than gaze tracking alone.

Further improvements were presented by Alkhalisy et al. [20], who combined Dlib facial landmark detection with YOLO object detection to recognize abnormal student behaviors during online examinations. Their system detected facial orientation, eye movements, and prohibited objects in real time, resulting in more accurate behavioral assessment while maintaining efficient processing performance suitable for web-based environments.

Advances in multimodal artificial intelligence were further demonstrated by Bhogate et al. [21], who designed an AI-based examination proctoring system integrating face detection, eye tracking, head pose estimation, and mouth movement analysis. Their implementation showed that combining multiple behavioral indicators increased monitoring reliability and reduced false alarms compared with conventional webcam monitoring approaches.

Machine learning has also been applied to specialized online assessments. Ortín et al. [22] developed a deep learning assistant for online programming examinations that analyzed screenshot sequences using convolutional and recurrent neural networks. Their framework achieved a classification accuracy of more than 95%, demonstrating the effectiveness of AI-assisted monitoring in identifying unauthorized activities during programming examinations.

DER et al. [23] proposed a multimodal online examination monitoring framework that combined facial recognition, gaze estimation, posture analysis, and object detection into a unified system. Experimental evaluation showed that integrating these behavioral indicators enabled the system to detect suspicious activities with high accuracy while maintaining low false-positive rates.

Recent work has also explored lightweight intelligent monitoring suitable for real-time deployment. An AI-driven cheating detection framework [24] integrated eye movement analysis, head pose estimation, and YOLOv8-based mobile phone detection to generate immediate alerts whenever suspicious behaviors were identified. The study demonstrated that combining object detection with behavioral analysis improved the robustness of automated proctoring systems.

A more comprehensive multimodal framework was introduced by Naveen et al. [25] through the AutoOEP system. The proposed framework combined ArcFace identity verification, gaze estimation, head pose analysis, mouth movement detection, and YOLO-based prohibited object recognition, while an LSTM model calculated the probability of cheating in real time. The system achieved an overall classification accuracy of approximately 90.7%, demonstrating that integrating multiple artificial intelligence techniques provides an effective solution for maintaining academic integrity in online examinations.

These studies demonstrate that intelligent examination platforms have evolved from simple webcam monitoring into comprehensive multimodal systems capable of analyzing facial features, gaze direction, head movements, posture, and prohibited objects.
